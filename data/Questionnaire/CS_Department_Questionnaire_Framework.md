# CS College Department Selection Questionnaire
## Comprehensive Design & Validation Framework

---

## 1. QUESTIONNAIRE OBJECTIVES

### Primary Goal
To help first-year and undecided students self-identify the most suitable department among five specialized programs by measuring **preference fit** across intellectual style, work preferences, mathematical comfort, and career aspirations—rather than academic ability alone.

### Secondary Goals
- **Differential Accuracy:** Distinguish between students with overlapping interests (e.g., ML interest across CS/AI/DS, cybersecurity across CS/IT, data work across IS/DS/AI)
- **Student Empowerment:** Provide transparent, actionable feedback that helps students understand their profile across the five departments
- **Academic Planning:** Supply advisors with quantitative insight into student orientation to guide course selection and mentoring
- **Validity Evidence:** Establish baseline data for iterative refinement of the questionnaire

### Target Audience
- High school graduates applying to the college
- Current students considering late declaration or transfer
- Advising staff needing standardized assessment tool

## 2. CONSTRUCT MAP BY DEPARTMENT

### Artificial Intelligence (AI)
**Core Construct:** Theoretical depth in machine learning, cognition, and intelligent systems

**Key Distinguishing Factors:**
- Comfort with **formal mathematical proofs** and research-oriented thinking
- Fascination with **understanding intelligence at a theoretical level** (not just using tools)
- Interest in **research pathways** and potential graduate study
- Preference for **deep understanding of why algorithms work** over rapid implementation
- Exposure to or interest in **cognitive science** and learning theory

**Discriminating Questions:** Q2, Q6, Q8, Q12, Q16, Q20

**Affinity Indicators:**
- Strong agreement with "I want to know the mathematical foundation" (not just application)
- Chooses "Research scientist" or "PhD pathway" in career questions
- High interest in "Understanding how learning algorithms work"
- Prefers learning theory over practical implementation

---

### Computer Science (CS)
**Core Construct:** Breadth across computational systems from theory to application

**Key Distinguishing Factors:**
- Strong **coding and programming orientation** across multiple paradigms
- Interest in understanding **how systems work at multiple levels** (hardware to applications)
- Comfort with **abstract problem-solving** and algorithmic thinking
- Preference for **building systems from scratch** rather than configuration
- Interest in **software engineering** and full-stack development

**Discriminating Questions:** Q1, Q3, Q5, Q7, Q11, Q14, Q17

**Affinity Indicators:**
- High comfort coding; sees coding as primary professional activity
- Interested in OS, compilers, cloud architectures
- Enjoys "why does this work?" questions (theory + practice balance)
- Career vision: backend engineer, software architect, full-stack developer

---

### Computer Science (IT)
**Core Construct:** Hardware, infrastructure, networks, and physical systems

**Key Distinguishing Factors:**
- **Hardware and physical systems** are the primary fascination
- Interest in **networks, signals, and data transmission**
- Attraction to **embedded systems, IoT, microcontrollers**
- Comfort with **infrastructure, configuration, and operations**
- Career interest in **cybersecurity (infrastructure focus), network engineering, embedded systems**

**Discriminating Questions:** Q4, Q9, Q10, Q13, Q18

**Affinity Indicators:**
- Strong interest in "How do networks and electronics work?"
- Chooses hardware/infrastructure activities over coding-only tasks
- Attracted to cybersecurity from infrastructure/defense angle
- Career: network engineer, security analyst, infrastructure specialist

---

### Data Science (DS)
**Core Construct:** Mathematical optimization, quantitative modeling, and decision science

**Key Distinguishing Factors:**
- **Mathematical sophistication** (proofs, calculus, linear algebra)
- Strong in **statistics and probability**
- Preference for **optimization** (finding best solutions) over computation
- Interest in **modeling, simulation, and scenario analysis**
- Career aspiration toward **analytics, operations research, quantitative consulting**

**Discriminating Questions:** Q2, Q6, Q12, Q15, Q19

**Affinity Indicators:**
- Comfortable with formal proofs and mathematical derivations
- Interested in "optimization and modeling" over systems building
- Career vision: data scientist, operations analyst, management consultant
- High value placed on statistics and mathematical optimization

---

### Information Systems (IS)
**Core Construct:** Business processes, databases, organizational IT, and user-centered systems

**Key Distinguishing Factors:**
- **Business process and organizational understanding**
- Interest in **databases and data systems** (architecture, not just analysis)
- Preference for **systems analysis and requirements gathering**
- Comfort with **stakeholder engagement** and business translation
- Career focus on **enterprise IT, systems analysis, IT consulting**

**Discriminating Questions:** Q11, Q13, Q15, Q17, Q19

**Affinity Indicators:**
- Values understanding "how businesses operate"
- Interested in systems analysis and user requirements
- Career: systems analyst, database administrator, IT consultant
- Sees technology as **enabler of organizational goals**

## 3. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

### Five Dimensions of Measurement

#### Dimension 1: Mathematical Orientation
- **Spectrum:** Applied/Practical ↔ Theoretical/Proof-based
- **Departments:**
  - High Theory: AI, DS
  - Balanced: CS
  - Applied: IT, IS

#### Dimension 2: Coding & Abstraction
- **Spectrum:** No Coding ↔ Heavy Coding ↔ Systems-Level Programming
- **Departments:**
  - Heavy Programming: CS
  - Moderate: IT, AI, DS
  - Light: IS

#### Dimension 3: Hardware & Infrastructure
- **Spectrum:** Pure Software ↔ Integrated Hardware/Infrastructure Focus
- **Departments:**
  - Hardware-Primary: IT
  - Hardware-Integrated: CS (organization/architecture)
  - Software-Focused: AI, DS
  - Database/Application-Focused: IS

#### Dimension 4: Business & Organizational Orientation
- **Spectrum:** Pure Technology ↔ Technology + Business Context
- **Departments:**
  - Business-Integrated: IS, DS
  - Pure Technology: CS, IT, AI

#### Dimension 5: Career Vision & Research Orientation
- **Spectrum:** Industry Engineering ↔ Research/Advanced Study
- **Departments:**
  - Research-Oriented: AI
  - Industry-Oriented: CS, IT, IS
  - Flexible (Both Paths): DS

## 4. ITEM DESIGN PRINCIPLES

### Design Checklist Applied to All Items

✓ **Clear and Unambiguous Language**
- Avoid discipline jargon in the question; define technical terms when necessary
- Use active, concrete language ("Do you enjoy writing code?" not "Is programming appealing to you?")
- One concept per question (no double-barreled questions)

✓ **Multiple Item Formats**
- **Likert Scale (5-point):** For measuring strength of agreement with statements about preferences
- **Ranking/Scenario Questions:** For situational preference (e.g., "Which activity appeals most?")
- **Career Pathway Questions:** Direct mapping to department trajectories

✓ **Balanced Representation**
- Each department receives 3-4 dedicated discrimination questions
- Questions span all five theoretical dimensions
- Avoid clustering items that will artificially correlate

✓ **Reversals & Negation Minimized**
- Most questions are positively phrased to avoid confusion
- Reverse-scored items used sparingly to break response patterns

✓ **Developmental Sensitivity**
- Appropriate for 17-22-year-old students; avoids overly sophisticated psychological language
- Acknowledges that interests evolve; focuses on **current preference**, not predicted mastery

## 5. SCORING METHODOLOGY

### Scoring Approach: Points-Based Multi-Dimensional Profile

Each answer is mapped to one or more departments with a point value:
- **3 points** = Strong alignment with that department
- **2 points** = Moderate alignment with that department
- **1 point** = Weak alignment with that department
- **0 points** = No alignment or neutral

### Final Scoring & Ranking

**Step 1:** Sum all points for each department across all 20 questions
- Range per department: 0–60 points (20 items × 3 max points)

**Step 2:** Calculate **raw profile score** for each department

**Step 3:** Normalize scores to **percentile or tier ranking** (e.g., "Strong Fit," "Moderate Fit," "Lower Fit")

**Step 4:** Generate **final ranking** from highest to lowest score, with ties handled as equal

### Example Scoring Output
```
Department Ranking:
1. Computer Science: 48/60 (Strong Fit) ████████
2. Data Science: 35/60 (Moderate Fit) █████
3. Artificial Intelligence: 28/60 (Lower Fit) ████
4. Information Systems: 22/60 (Lower Fit) ███
5. Information Technology: 18/60 (Minimal Fit) ██
```

### Interpretation Guidance for Advisors
- **Score ≥45:** Student strongly aligned; proactive recommendation
- **Score 30–44:** Student moderately aligned; supportive recommendation with awareness of adjacent areas
- **Score <30:** Consider alternative departments higher ranked

### Aggregate Validity Data
- Track distribution of scores within each class cohort
- Monitor students who score high on multiple departments (need conversation to disambiguate)
- Measure post-declaration regret rates annually to refine scoring weights

## 6. QUESTIONNAIRE ITEMS & EXPLANATIONS

### Question-by-Question Rationale

**Q1: "In your ideal computer science role, what appeals to you most?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Discriminates between **CS/IT (building/coding focus)** and **IS (organizational focus)**
- **Construct:** Work preference and career vision
- **Format:** Scenario choice (5 options)
- **Scoring:** Heavy CS weighting for "building complex systems"; IT for "hardware/infrastructure"; IS for "supporting business"

---

**Q2: "How comfortable are you with formal mathematical proofs (deriving formulas, proving theorems)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Strongest discriminator between **AI/DS (theory)** and **IT/IS (applied)**
- **Construct:** Mathematical orientation
- **Format:** Likert 5-point scale (Very Uncomfortable → Very Comfortable)
- **Scoring:** Comfort correlates strongly with AI and DS; discomfort with IT and IS

---

**Q3: "How much coding do you want to do as a percentage of your professional work?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Distinguishes **CS/AI (high coding)** from **IS (moderate)** and **DS (varied)**
- **Construct:** Coding orientation
- **Format:** Ranking or percentage-based scale
- **Scoring:** High coding → CS; moderate → AI/IT; low → IS

---

**Q4: "How interested are you in understanding how computer hardware, networks, and electronics work?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **Primary discriminator for IT**
- **Construct:** Hardware/infrastructure orientation
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** High interest = IT boost

---

**Q5: "Do you enjoy solving algorithmic puzzles where you design elegant, efficient solutions?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Core to **CS** and secondary to **AI/DS**
- **Construct:** Abstract problem-solving preference
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Aligns with CS and AI (where algorithms underpin both)

---

**Q6: "When you learn something new in tech, are you satisfied with understanding *how* to use it, or do you need to understand *why* it works mathematically?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Separates **AI/DS (need to understand why)** from **CS (both ok)** and **IT/IS (happy with how)**
- **Construct:** Theoretical vs. practical orientation
- **Format:** Choice / Likert scale
- **Scoring:** "Why" orientation → AI/DS

---

**Q7: "How important is it to you to understand the entire stack (from hardware through applications)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Characteristic of **CS** ambition; less so for specialists
- **Construct:** Breadth vs. depth preference
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Breadth desire → CS

---

**Q8: "Are you interested in research and potentially pursuing graduate school (Master's/PhD)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **AI >> others** for research pathway
- **Construct:** Career trajectory (academic vs. industry)
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Research interest → AI, secondary DS

---

**Q9: "Which of these appeals to you most: networks, cybersecurity (infrastructure focus), embedded systems, IoT?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **Primary IT question**
- **Construct:** Domain preference
- **Format:** Multiple choice (select all or rank)
- **Scoring:** All options → IT boost

---

**Q10: "Do you enjoy hands-on work with physical systems (tinkering, building circuits, configuring devices)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **IT-specific** vs. pure software-focused
- **Construct:** Hands-on physical orientation
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Yes → IT

---

**Q11: "How interested are you in understanding how businesses operate (processes, decisions, workflows)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **IS > DS**; distinguishes from pure tech focus
- **Construct:** Organizational/business orientation
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** High interest → IS

---

**Q12: "When faced with a problem, are you more drawn to: building a solution that works, or optimizing to find the *best* solution?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Distinguishes **CS (building works) vs. DS (optimizing best)**
- **Construct:** Optimization vs. engineering mindset
- **Format:** Choice or scale
- **Scoring:** Optimization → DS; building → CS

---

**Q13: "How important is it to you that the technology you develop improves user experience or organizational efficiency?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **IS-specific** value on business impact
- **Construct:** User/business-centered thinking
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** High importance → IS

---

**Q14: "Do you want to be able to build entire systems end-to-end, or do you prefer to specialize deeply in one area?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **CS = end-to-end**; others = specialists
- **Construct:** Breadth vs. depth in career
- **Format:** Choice or scale
- **Scoring:** End-to-end → CS; specialization → others

---

**Q15: "How comfortable are you with statistics and probability as a foundation for your work?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Essential for **DS/IS**; less so for CS/IT/AI (though AI needs it)
- **Construct:** Statistical thinking
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Comfort → DS/IS

---

**Q16: "Would you be interested in understanding how machine learning algorithms learn and why they work (not just using existing tools)?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Separates **AI (theoretical interest)** from **CS (applied use)** and **DS (application to data)**
- **Construct:** ML theory vs. ML application
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Theoretical interest → AI; applied → CS; data-focused → DS

---

**Q17: "How would you describe your preferred work environment?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Capture career & team context preferences
- **Construct:** Career/environment fit
- **Format:** Ranking or multiple choice
- **Scoring:** Varied by option; "working with a team on business solutions" → IS; "research lab" → AI; "startups" → CS

---

**Q18: "Which cybersecurity focus appeals more: defending against attacks at the infrastructure/network level, or securing applications and data?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Disambiguates **IT vs. CS** for security-interested students
- **Construct:** Security domain preference
- **Format:** Choice
- **Scoring:** Infrastructure → IT; applications → CS

---

**Q19: "Do you see yourself using advanced mathematical models (optimization, simulation, statistical modeling) in your work?"**
- **Why This Matters:** **DS primary**; secondary IS
- **Construct:** Mathematical modeling preference
- **Format:** Likert 5-point
- **Scoring:** Yes → DS/IS

---

**Q20: "In 10 years, which role is closest to your aspiration?"**
- **Why This Matters:** Direct career pathway alignment
- **Construct:** Long-term career goal
- **Format:** Multiple choice (one per department)
- **Scoring:** Direct mapping; 3 points for chosen department

## 7. VALIDATION PLAN

### 7.1 Internal Validity (Before Deployment)

#### Content Validity
- **Expert Review Panel:** 5 faculty members (1 per department) review each question for:
  - Accuracy of departmental alignment
  - Absence of jargon or bias
  - Clarity of wording
  - Avoidance of double-barreling
- **Revision Threshold:** ≥80% agreement required; revise or replace items not meeting threshold

#### Construct Validity
- **Mapping Review:** Confirm that each question maps to intended theoretical dimension
  - Create construct-to-question matrix
  - Ensure each dimension covered by ≥2 items
  - Check for redundancy or clustering

#### Face Validity
- **Student Pilot (n=15-20):** Ask diverse students to complete the questionnaire and provide feedback:
  - "Was the question clear?"
  - "Did the answer options feel complete?"
  - "Did any questions feel biased or confusing?"
- **Revise ambiguous or confusing items based on feedback**

---

### 7.2 Predictive Validity (Post-Deployment Monitoring)

#### Declaration Matching
- **Track:** Do high-scoring students in department X actually declare department X?
- **Baseline Goal:** ≥60% match rate between top-score department and actual declaration
- **Action:** If <60%, investigate which questions are weak discriminators and revise

#### Regret Tracking
- **Survey:** 3 months after declaration, ask students:
  - "How satisfied are you with your department choice?"
  - "Do you wish you'd chosen differently?"
- **Analysis:** Compare satisfaction of "high-match" vs. "lower-match" groups
- **Action:** If satisfaction doesn't correlate with match score, recalibrate weighting

---

### 7.3 Reliability (Internal Consistency & Stability)

#### Internal Consistency (Cronbach's Alpha per subscale)
- Compute Cronbach's alpha for department-specific subscales (items designed to measure that department)
- **Target:** α ≥ 0.65 per department
- **Action:** If α <0.65, consider removing or revising redundant/weak items within that subscale

#### Test-Retest Stability
- **Administer twice** to n=30 students with 2-week interval
- **Compute correlation** between first and second administrations (Pearson r)
- **Target:** r ≥ 0.70
- **Action:** If r <0.70, items are ambiguous or unstable; revise wording or scoring

---

### 7.4 Discriminant Validity

#### Inter-Department Correlation Analysis
- **Compute Spearman correlations** between department scores across the full student sample
- **Expected:** Low-to-moderate correlations between unrelated departments
  - CS ↔ IT: low-moderate (both systems-focused)
  - CS ↔ IS: low (different orientations)
  - AI ↔ DS: moderate (both math-heavy)
  - AI ↔ CS: moderate-high (overlap in coding)
- **Action:** If correlations are unexpectedly high (>0.80), questions may conflate constructs

---

### 7.5 Practical Validation: Advisor Utility

#### Usability Testing with Academic Advisors
- **Conduct focus group (n=4-5 advisors)** to review:
  - Clarity of score output
  - Actionability of recommendations
  - Time required to administer and interpret
- **Revise presentation of results based on feedback**

#### Advisor Agreement Rate
- **Present results to advisors blind** (without seeing student names)
- **Ask:** "Based on this score profile, which department would you recommend?"
- **Compare:** Advisor recommendation vs. questionnaire's top-ranked department
- **Target:** ≥70% agreement
- **Action:** If <70%, explore misaligned questions or scoring weights

---

### 7.6 Iterative Refinement (Years 1–3)

#### Annual Cycles
1. **Year 1:** Administer to full cohort; collect declaration and 3-month satisfaction data
2. **Analyze:** Identify weak items (low discrimination, unexpected correlations, low satisfaction prediction)
3. **Year 1.5:** Revise questionnaire based on findings; update scoring weights if needed
4. **Year 2:** Administer revised version; compare validation metrics to baseline
5. **Year 3:** Lock in final version; establish ongoing monitoring system

#### Version Management
- Maintain previous versions for research comparison
- Document all changes with rationale
- Communicate updates to advisors and students

## 8. APPENDIX: GLOSSARY OF CONSTRUCTS

- **Mathematical Orientation:** The student's comfort with and preference for abstract, proof-based vs. applied, practical-focused mathematics
- **Coding Orientation:** The extent to which the student wants programming to be their primary professional activity
- **Hardware/Infrastructure Focus:** Interest in understanding and working with physical systems (networks, electronics, embedded systems)
- **Organizational/Business Orientation:** The student's value placed on understanding and improving organizational processes and user needs
- **Optimization vs. Engineering Mindset:** Preference for finding the mathematically best solution vs. building systems that work reliably
- **Research vs. Industry Focus:** Career aspiration toward academic/research pathways vs. industry engineering roles
- **Theoretical vs. Applied Thinking:** Needing to understand why something works mathematically vs. being satisfied with knowing how to use it
